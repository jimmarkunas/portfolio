DIRECTV Everywhere: Turning a Marketing Promise Into a Real Streaming Product

DIRECTV had already bought national ad spots promising "Everywhere" access — but the actual product was still half-baked. Context & Stakes

Before Everywhere, DIRECTV was still fundamentally a set-top-box business. Subscribers could watch on the TV the dish was wired to, maybe dabble with a clunky VOD experience — but there was no sense that your content followed you from screen to screen. Meanwhile, Netflix, HBO GO, and others were training customers to expect exactly that. Marketing had already launched a big national "Watch Anywhere" campaign before the product was ready, putting engineering — and me — in the hot seat.

What DIRECTV Needed

  • Deliver on the national "Watch Anywhere" campaign already in market

The Stakes

  • Marketing had promised "Everywhere" before engineering had built it — reputation on the line

  • Netflix and HBO GO were setting the bar — a broken experience would make DIRECTV look worse than nothing

The Challenge

Technical Blockers

  • No shared identity or entitlement model across device platforms
  • Linear and VOD catalogs disconnected — no coherent search or discovery
  • DRM and rights integration required per-network negotiation on top of engineering work
  • Six device surfaces, zero shared UX patterns — every team reinventing independently

Organizational Blockers

  • 30+ teams — programming, devices, marketing, identity, platform engineering — all misaligned
  • Marketing had launched "Watch Anywhere" before engineering had a plan
  • No PM owned the cross-platform product — just local OKRs and team silos
  • Design vs. engineering conflict escalating — my manager walked out of one meeting

The Core Tension: DIRECTV leadership needed a product that felt simple to subscribers — even though the rights, device rules, and entitlement logic behind it were extraordinarily messy. That gap between "simple for the user" and "complex for everyone building it" was exactly where I sat.

Brought In — June 2012

Marketing had already run national "Watch Anywhere" spots. Product was still half-built. I was brought in as PM with a founder's background in internet TV (ZEVO) — knowing how streaming should feel and what it takes to ship cross-device experiences under pressure.

PRDs, UX Model & Cross-Team Governance

Wrote the PRDs and flows defining how you move from login to content to player across all six surfaces. Navigation, browse patterns, and the player model aligned from day one. Created the release playbook: who owns what, what "ready" means, and how trade-offs get made across 30+ teams.

Identity & Entitlement Model

Partnered with identity teams to define the SSO model — DIRECTV credentials, tokens, device limits handled as one unified flow. Sign in once, package recognized, watchable content surfaced. DRM and rights rules integrated without making subscribers fight the system.

Catalog, Search & Discovery

Connected linear and VOD catalogs into a single coherent experience. Search, carousels, and editorial collections built around real viewer behavior — "continue watching," "premium you already pay for" — not network folder dumps. Live + VOD, not just back-catalog, made DTVE feel like TV.

Launch Across All Platforms

Web, iOS, Android, Roku, Fire TV, and Apple TV all shipped. 5M+ downloads in Year 1. The Hollywood Reporter, Engadget, MIT Technology Review, and Yahoo News covered the launch as a milestone in TV's shift to streaming — one of the first true "TV Everywhere" products from a major satellite provider.

Blueprint for Future Streaming — June 2013

The DTVE architecture — shared identity, unified catalog, cross-device UX patterns, release playbook — became the foundation for DIRECTV's later streaming-only offerings. The patterns we established before "streaming wars" was a headline are what everyone else now takes for granted.

Before DTVE

  • Subscribers tethered to one TV — no true cross-device streaming, just limited VOD on PC
  • "Watch Anywhere" marketing promise out ahead of reality — risking trust and call-center overload
  • Each device team with its own roadmap and UX assumptions — no single cross-platform product view
  • Auth and entitlements opaque — multiple steps, felt bolted-on, dead-ends common

After DTVE

  • Single product spanning web and connected devices — millions of downloads in Y1 validating the strategy
  • Unified SSO and entitlement model — clear what you can watch, successful play starts up
  • Cross-device UX parity — UI updates rollout consistently across all platforms
  • DTVE architecture became the foundation for DIRECTV's later streaming-only offerings

The Real Value: DIRECTV looked and behaved like a modern streaming company at a time when most pay-TV providers were still treating apps as side projects. The patterns we established — authenticated streaming tied to pay-TV subscriptions, cross-device parity, shared entitlement model — are what everyone else now takes for granted.
