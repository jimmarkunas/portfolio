The Product

CWG wasn't a blog that got big. It was designed from day one as a multi-surface content product ‚Äî the same stories, photography, and video flowing through a WordPress site, a native iOS app, a YouTube channel, and social feeds. I owned every product decision: information architecture, feature prioritization, cross-surface asset reuse, and basic analytics.

WordPress Site Native iOS App YouTube Channel Short-Form Video Social Distribution Shared Asset Library

Product Thesis

  • Legitimate, not scrappy. The experience had to feel comparable to Rolling Stone and Stereogum ‚Äî not like a fan site.
  • Mobile and video first. Most music outlets were still treating apps as afterthoughts. We shipped iOS and original video from the start.
  • Content is the platform. The same assets ‚Äî photos, interviews, features ‚Äî had to work across every surface without re-production.
  • Artist access as a moat. Being reliable and respectful earned long-term access that money couldn't buy.

What Shipped

  • Site: Category structure tuned for bands, tours, festivals, and long-form features ‚Äî not just a reverse-chronological feed.
  • iOS app: Native app surfacing new stories, photo galleries, and embedded video directly on readers' phones.
  • Video: Original interviews and mini-documentaries paired with every major feature ‚Äî music television on the internet, before that phrase existed.
  • Photography: Consistent, high-quality live photography as a defining brand asset across all surfaces.

What Worked. What Didn't.

CWG was honest about its own failure mode: we knew how to get attention, we did not know how to turn attention into a sustainable business. That distinction shaped every product role I've had since.

‚úì What Worked

  • Building a credible brand with zero funding ‚Äî product discipline mattered more than budget
  • Recruiting and managing a global creative team on mission and access alone
  • Shipping early on new surfaces: iOS app and original video before most music outlets
  • Earning long-term artist and label access by being reliable and respectful ‚Äî not by spending
  • Treating the "zine" as a product: surfaces, UX, distribution, and data ‚Äî not just articles

‚úó What Didn't

  • Monetization as an afterthought ‚Äî revenue was never a first-class product problem
  • Over-reliance on display ads in a weak early-2010s digital ad market
  • No clear path to paying core contributors, which led to burnout and churn
  • Underinvestment in owned channels: email lists and membership were left on the table
  • Treating "people love this" as a proxy for "this can support a team" ‚Äî it can't

The central lesson: Attention is not a business model. We were disciplined about building an audience; we were not disciplined about designing a revenue engine with the same rigor. My next venture will treat unit economics and product incentives as first-class problems from day one ‚Äî not as something to figure out once the audience is there.

Legacy & What Came Next

CWG never became a sustainable company, and that's precisely why it belongs in this portfolio. We built a real product, earned real scale, and made the deliberate decision to shut it down rather than run indefinitely on volunteer labor. I left with a clearer picture of what it takes to turn creative energy into a viable business.
